OVERVIEW

ROLE

This project was the outcome of a semester long project within the course Principles of Interaction Design. While creating this project, my team utilized formal interaction design processes to brainstorm, map, create, and test our product. I led UX Design and Research while my teammate handled Graphic Design.

This was my first project using a formal design process, and though the outcome is not my best work, I am proud of what this project taught me and how it has shaped my goals and hopes in college and my career.

TOOLS USED

CHALLENGE

Community Farmers Market was looking for a way to better market it’s large product life cycle. With its extensive reach, it supplies items from farm products to vendor prepared goods. Each week these items could be purchased at weekend markets, sent to MARTA markets where they are sold for discounted prices, or be moved back to compost and returned to farms. Some vendors care deeply about sustainable packaging and practices while others aren’t as knowledgeable. Our goal was to build an interactive tool to help explain and educate about the market from a holistic perspective.

The Process

Using Don Norman's Design Thinking method, our process helped us to more fully empathize with and understand our users.



Empathize and Define

This phase included client conversations, user analysis, persona creation, identifying user goals, and brainstorming.



Personas

CFM is looking to communicate to vendors and customers how the market and the larger product lifecycle operate while better encouraging vendors to commit to certified organic food, locally grown produce, compostable packaging, and decreasing food miles.

User Goals

Identified through storyboarding as well as conversations with client


Consumer

  • To clearly identify which businesses operate with sustainable practices
  • To see more transparency from vendors throughout the market
  • To have a consolidated source to learn about the sustainable practices each vendor implements

Vendor

  • To celebrate and display their own achievements within sustainability
  • To learn more about how to implement new and better sustainable practices
  • To create a network of vendors committing to sustainability for sharing ideas, products, and support

Solution

We decided to create a resource to engage and educate vendors, consumers, and members of the community about sustainability. Our goal was to celebrate and award the vendors who have demonstrated sustainable practices based on the product life cycle.

⚠️ Speed Bump ⚠️


After discussing this concept, our client loved the idea of rewarding vendors but was reluctant to use technology to do so. We had to divert our app's focus from a vendor portal towards a more consumer focused educational platform to satisfy our client and meet the technological requirements of the course.




Medium Fidelity Prototyping


Our app shifted more towards an educational/informative consumer and vendor facing webpage.

Test: Wizard of Oz Method

With a WOZ style approach, we were able to have our website evaluated from different vendor and consumer point of views (each participant acting as a different persona) while also curating the intended interactions as the wizard.



Process


(4 student evaluators)
Design and Computational Media students were paired with one of the above personas. They were asked to familiarize themselves with the characteristics, motivations, goals, and frustrations of their assigned persona, and were asked to think from those perspectives as they were walked through our prototype. Evaluators then explained their thought processes, likes/dislikes, and any confusion they may have had during the prototype walkthrough.



Rationale


We selected this method to discover how our prototype may suit the different personas and address their specific goals.
Since the prototype was not fully operational, WOZ testing also allowed us to guide the users through the prototype to ensure the most realistic experience



Key Weaknesses Discovered:

  • Lack of “call to action” or participation element for non-vendors
  • No troubleshooting, FAQ, help section, or contact information for our less tech savvy users
  • Interactions felt disjointed and non intuitive on certain pages

Improvements to make:

  • Implement consistent design elements in terms of color scheme, typography, and graphics
  • Re-design the flow of information to intuitively guide users through the education process
  • Incorporate a “call to action” or way for the community members to feel more engaged in the website and initiative

Reflection

My main regret with this project is shifting our design from a vendor portal to an educational website. We made this change after our client advised us against creating technology dependent on vendor interaction. In a professional environment, we likely would have had access to conversation with vendors and also a much larger time frame. In that case, upon receiving pushback from our client, I would have conducted user testing to truly assess the vendors' receptiveness to an interactive app. Because that was not a possibility within the confines of a semester-long course, we trusted our clients' knowledge of vendors and rerouted the project. Looking back I'm hesitant about whether we made the right change. The switch stifled interaction. In the end, we created not much more than an interactive PowerPoint.



This is one of those projects where I look at the final outcome and feel a bit disappointed. Nevertheless, I wanted it in my portfolio because I am proud of the research and design process that went into it. This was one of the first formal design processes I got to be a part of and I distinctly remember experiencing the feeling of 'wow, this is where I'm supposed to be'. So even though I don't claim this as my best work, I'm proud of what it taught me and continues to teach me about myself and what kind of designer I want to be.
